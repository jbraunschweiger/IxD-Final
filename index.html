Oasis Case Study

Overview

Context

In their third year, Washington University in St. Louis medical students are required to complete different clerkships: neurology, psychiatry, pediatrics, Ob/GYN, surgery and medicine. One requirement for these real-world experiences is in the form of a “requirement log,” which is meant to ensure that each student is meeting the necessary criteria in each clerkships. The tool used to record these logs is Oasis, an online educational program that allows admistration to review the students progress.

Problem Statements

How can we ehance the Oasis requirement log system to improve the user’s eduational/ clerkship experience?

How might we create a digital experience that encourages easy and reliable data entry for very busy third year medical students? (class question outlined in assignment)

Name: Riley

Scope

Project Scope

The objective of this project was to redesign OASIS, a program used by third-year medical students to input patient logs from clinical rotations. The project was tasked to us by our partners from the medical school at WashU and we gleaned insights from our partners and third-year medical students in order to construct our solution. Our solution to the project goal was to create a mobile app with a primary focus on streamlining the process of inputting patient data and making requirement checklists easily accessible and understandable.

Constraints

  • Due to the pandemic, this course was fully remote (online only), which meant we were not able to tour or observe Washu medical facilites.
  • Due to HIPAA regulations, we were not allowed direct access to the Oasis platform. Instead we were given brief presentations on the primary functions, which meant we only had a basic understanding of program.

Our lack of knowledge in the operations of medical school meant we needed to establish a basic understanding of the third year clerkship program. Previous Next

Our initial findings determined that there were a few essential factors to consider:

  1. Ease of use
  2. Time efficiency
  3. Depth of learning expereince

At this point, we were interested in exploring ways of changing up the journey map, and getting Oasis users to input logs or take detailed notes throughout the day. A mobile experience, potentially one that leveraged hospital bracelet QR codes, seemed like a great way to tackle this.


Prototyping

Using the insights from the persona, journey maps, and interviews, we created sitemaps and lo-fidelity wireframes. We created a sitemap for the entire mobile experience as well as specifically for the patient logs.

The wireframes were created based on what we concluded were the most important features of OASIS - the patient logs, requirement checklist, ability to easily input patient data, and reminders and notifications about upcoming deadlines.

With our idea coming together in the form of sitemaps and wireframes, we created a storyboard to illustrate how our design could be beneficial in real world use.


Refined Design

We took our initial designs back to our original interviewess in order to get their feedback. Our key takeaways from this round of feedback:

  1. Must ensure HIPAA compliance
  2. Experience avaliable on both mobile and desktop devices
  3. Establishing a notification student to reinforce requirements
  4. Include an option for manual data entry

Keeping this feedback in mind, we refined our prototypes both in terms of their features and visuals. The following are our final prototypes with descriptions explaining why and where they were included in the design.


QR Code

The scanning page for the OASIS app. The bottom 5 icons navigate the user’s to Oasis’s other features (calendar, patient logs, requirement checklist, settings) and the top rectangle presents a “done” button. The large middle square is the camera view to scan the patient information.

QR Code Results

The editable patient log portal. After scanning the patient information, the student will be taken to this page, which shows the information obtained by the scan. There is a save button at the top right to immediately save and exit out of the portal, or the student may edit/add information in the log before saving. To ensure patient privacy under HIPAA, the patient’s name is not recorded in the patient log. Instead, each patient recieves a unique and nonidentifiable ID number. The user can add tags to logs to identify content as belonging to a particular category or topic.


Patient Log

The patient log portal. After saving the information obtained by the scan and/or saving any edited information, the student will be taken to this page. This is the layout of the saved patient portal and what the administrative view looks like as well. The student also has the option to edit any of the logs from this view by clicking a particular log. There is also a search feature to look up previously completed patient logs.

Requirement checklist

The requirements page. This page contains a list of the requirements the students still has to complete as well as their completed requirements. This page can be accessed through the patient log portal.


Student profile

The profile page for the student. This page contains the student’s profile picture, login name, censored password, and notifications from the app for the student.


Project Retrospective

Lessons Learned

  • The difficulty of thinking about both the student’s side of OASIS and the administrative side and making sure both parts work together
  • The importance of creating an app that is curated for a specific persona (a busy medical student in this case)
  • The value in asking for insight and feedback from the client and target audience

Next Steps

  • Expand the calendar and questions features
  • Make a desktop-friendly model of the current mobile app
  • Integrate more ways to input patient data if scanning is not available at a hospital
  • Have a more categorized way of organizing patient logs and requirement checklists
  • Have students use a protype in a real rotation
